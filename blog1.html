Why “Perfect Practice” is a Myth

We often hear the phrase “practice makes perfect,” but for most musicians, “perfect” quickly becomes a trap. It creates pressure to sound flawless every single time, and when that doesn’t happen, frustration takes over.

In reality, practice is rarely clean. Some days your voice or hands cooperate, some days they don’t. You might repeat a phrase ten times and still feel unsure. That doesn’t mean the session failed — it simply means you’re in the middle of the process.

Instead of chasing perfection, it’s more helpful to focus on small, repeatable improvements. Did your timing get a little more stable? Did you understand a phrase slightly better? Did your body feel more relaxed while playing or singing? These are all wins, even if the final output still isn’t “stage ready.”

Over time, these small improvements stack up into something big. What looks like “effortless” performance from the outside is usually the result of hundreds of imperfect, honest practice sessions that nobody sees.

So the next time a practice session feels messy, remember: the goal isn’t to be perfect today. The goal is to show up, listen carefully, and move one step forward — even if it’s a tiny one.
